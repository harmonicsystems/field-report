# Village of Kinderhook — primary source snapshots

## Files

### `2008-10_historic-preservation-overlay-district-review.pdf`

A single-page map titled **"Village of Kinderhook — Historic Preservation
Overlay District"** dated 2008-10. Produced by Community Planning &
Environmental Associates (Nan Stolzenburg, AICP) with GIS by Don Meltz.

**Why it matters.** This is the clearest published depiction we have of the
two distinct historic boundaries that govern Village properties — and they
are *not* the same shape:

1. **Historic Preservation Overlay District** (hatched diagonal lines) —
   the **locally-regulated** district referenced in § 75-5 and on the
   Village Zoning Map (Ch. 130, Attachment 4). This is the boundary that
   determines whether a property owner needs a **certificate of
   appropriateness** from the Historic Preservation Commission to alter
   exterior features. It is concentrated on the historic core: Broad St,
   Hudson St, the Albany Ave / US-9 spine through the village center, and
   adjacent blocks (Maiden Ln, Church St, Silvester St, Rothermel area).

2. **National Register Village Historic District** (solid pink fill) —
   the **federal** listing on the National Register of Historic Places.
   This is much larger: it extends south to and along Kinderhook Creek,
   covers the southern village agricultural lands, and reaches further
   north along Albany Ave. The NRHP listing has **no regulatory effect on
   private alterations**; it makes properties eligible for federal historic
   tax credits and triggers federal Section 106 review only when federal
   funding or permits are involved.

The map's title — *"Historic Preservation Overlay District Review"* — and
its date (2008) suggest it was produced as a planning-process artifact
during a review or amendment of the local overlay; this is consistent with
the L.L. amendment history shown on Ch. 75 / Ch. 130.

**The civic-legibility finding.** Residents commonly believe "my house is
in the historic district" because the National Register area is large and
visible (in tax-credit program lists, in the village identity). But what
governs them — what triggers HPC review — is the much smaller hatched
local overlay. The two boundaries should be published side-by-side with
clear regulatory labels, which is what this map already does and what our
report should carry forward.
